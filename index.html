Book Rapport

Range

Why Generalists Triumph in a Specialized World

  • Career
  • Motivational
  • Non-Fiction

"Individuals are capable of more creative integration of diverse experiences than teams are."

Range was recommended to me numerous times by my husband who regularly raved about it. In a world where weâ€™re constantly told in our early years to pick a vocation and specialize in it in order to be successful, this book was a breath of fresh air for those of us with varied interests in a broad spectrum of industries and fields, who struggle to find the appeal of committing to only one. As I myself have worked in libraries, processed food factories, a trucking company, in restaurants from pub-style to fine-dining, in childcare, and in broadcasting, this book was a slam dunk for me.

With examples of both the power of specialization and the power of generalization, Epstein makes a strong case for those who have unconventional career backgrounds and can therefore draw from a pool of knowledge not available to the specialist. As explained, while the specialists world gets deeper, it tends to get more narrow, and this is where generalists excel.

Daily Rituals

How Artists Work

  • Artists/Creatives
  • Inspiration
  • Non-Fiction

"Merde! I absolutely detest all openings and parties! They're commercial, political, and everybody talks too much. They get on my tits!"

Definitely in my top 5 all-time favourite books,Rituals Delves into the private lives of artists, creatives, and great minds alike to shed light on what their daily routine was like during their more productive years. It is an illuminating spread, with lots of drinking, amphetamines, and long walks. Ernest Hemingway, Sigmund Freud, Gertrude Stein, Henri Voltaire, and Jane Austen are among the great names giving us a delicious glimpse of their daily humanity.

I make a point to read this book at least once a year to remind myself of the varied paths of creative discipline required to make great work.
